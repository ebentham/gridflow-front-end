The question

What will GB demand be tomorrow?

Given hourly history of national demand and a calendar of UK working days, school terms and bank holidays, produce a calibrated distribution over each of the next 48 half-hourly settlement periods. Calibrated means the empirical coverage of the predicted p10–p90 band should land near 80%; the p50 should match realised median absolute error competitive with naïve seasonal persistence; and quantile crossings should be rare.

The model is intentionally small and feature-light — a baseline against which to measure later additions (weather features, REMIT outages, market-driven behavioural shifts). It is also the input distribution for the residual-demand sampler that feeds the fundamentals SMP forecaster, so its calibration matters beyond its standalone accuracy.

Feature pipeline

All features defined in gridflow_models.estimators.day_ahead.features.demand_pipeline. Required lookback: 168h (7 days). Stateless transformers — refit on every fold.

  • CalendarFeature hour, settlement period, day of week, month, weekend flag, UK bank-holiday flag, sin/cos cyclical encodings on hour and DoW.
  • LagFeature · 24h National demand at the same settlement period yesterday. Captures intra-day shape persistence.
  • LagFeature · 48h Same period two days ago. Slight redundancy with 24h lag — kept for the Sun→Mon and Fri→Sat transitions where the 24h lag breaks weekly seasonality.
  • LagFeature · 168h Same period last week. The dominant weekly-seasonality feature.
  • RollingMean · 24h Recent-level signal; smooths over period-level noise. min_periods=12.
  • RollingMean · 168h Weekly mean — slow trend signal across regime shifts (e.g. mild winter, school holidays). min_periods=48.
Estimator

One LightGBM per quantile.

Independent LGBMRegressor(objective='quantile', alpha=q) for each of seven target quantiles. No joint-distribution constraint at training time — the dominant pattern in the energy-forecasting literature (Marcjasz 2022, Lago 2021). Quantile crossings, which independent estimators occasionally produce at low row counts, are repaired post-hoc by row-sorting the prediction matrix. The MonotonicityGate reports the pre-sort crossing rate; in the latest fold it sat at 0.3%.

The estimator is pure: it sees a TrainingSet with available_at and target_available_at already enforced — no leakage paths in or out — and returns a tidy Forecast object with explicit delivery_times, quantile keys, market, product, currency, and units. The orchestrator owns the lifecycle.

# Fit and forecast one fold — orchestrator-driven
from gridflow_models import TrainingOrchestrator, ValidationConfig
from gridflow_models.estimators.day_ahead import LGBMQuantileEstimator, demand_pipeline

orchestrator = TrainingOrchestrator(
    estimator=LGBMQuantileEstimator(num_leaves=63, learning_rate=0.05, n_estimators=300),
    pipeline=demand_pipeline(),
    validation=ValidationConfig(splitter="expanding_window", n_folds=6),
    target="national_demand_mw",
)

result = orchestrator.fit(start="2019-01-01", end="2026-04-01")
forecast = orchestrator.predict_quantiles(as_of="2026-04-29T23:00Z", horizon="24h")
# forecast.quantiles → {'q_0.05': [...], 'q_0.5': [...], 'q_0.95': [...]}

Why expanding-window?

Demand-driving regimes shift slowly. The 2020 COVID shock and the 2022 energy-price episode both move the level; expanding-window keeps prior regimes in the training set instead of dropping them in a sliding window — which matters because the 168h lag has weeks of long memory.

Embargo: 0d. The 24h horizon and immediate-availability target make a cross-fold leakage gap unnecessary. The TrainingSet constructor handles the strict target_available_at check inside each fold.

Reading the table

A well-calibrated 90% interval should contain the realised value 90% of the time. 89.2% is comfortably inside the ±5pp tolerance — the model neither over-confidently narrows the band nor pads it.

The 50% interval is slightly wide (52.1% vs 50%) — preferable to the alternative of underestimating uncertainty. Per ADR-019, no post-hoc rescaling is applied; instead, the calibration is fed forward to MAPIE conformalisation in the downstream stack model.

Two peaks, two regimes

Errors cluster around the morning ramp (07:00–09:00) and the evening peak (17:00–19:00). Both are structurally hard — small shifts in wake-up and dinner timing translate to large MW differences against a 30 GW baseline. The 168h lag captures most of it; what's left is weather-driven.

Off-peak nights (00:00–05:00) are nearly trivial — flat demand, small dispersion, and the lag features carry virtually all the signal.

Next iteration. Adding Open-Meteo 2m-temperature and a 24h temperature-anomaly feature should compress the evening tail. Tracked as F5-F.
Reproduce it

Fetch the same data, locally.

Every input to the model comes from a single silver-layer table. The training query is short enough to fit on screen. 

-- 7y of half-hourly national demand, ready for the feature pipeline
SELECT
  settlement_dt                            AS event_time,
  national_demand_mw,
  ingested_at                              AS available_at
FROM silver.indo
WHERE settlement_dt >= '2019-01-01'
  AND run_type = 'OUTTURN'            -- exclude forecast revisions
ORDER BY 1;

Pass the resulting frame to TrainingSet(...) and the orchestrator handles fold construction, target availability checks, feature fitting per fold, and gate evaluation. Roughly 90 seconds end-to-end on a 2024 laptop; 4–8s per fold dominated by LightGBM training.

Caveats

What this model isn't.

No weather features.

Today's model is calendar + lag only. Open-Meteo grid features are next on the list (temperature, wind chill, cloud cover). Until then, residuals on hot/cold tails will be wider than they need to be.

National only.

indo is a national sum. Zonal demand (B-zones) is not modelled. For nodal price work the model is a baseline at best — and not the right primitive at worst.

Quantiles are independent.

We don't model the joint distribution across delivery times. For 48-period dispatch problems this matters; the downstream ResidualDemandSampler currently assumes marginal independence (ADR-039). A copula layer is deferred.
